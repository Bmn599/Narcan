Recognize the signs of an opioid overdose

If you suspect an overdose, call 911. Then follow the steps below.

Common signs

  • Unresponsive, cannot be woken up
  • Slow, shallow, or no breathing, gurgling or snoring sounds
  • Pale, clammy skin, blue or gray lips or fingertips
  • Pinpoint pupils

What to do first

  • Call 911 and state possible opioid overdose
  • Lay the person on their back and clear the airway
  • Be ready to start rescue breathing if needed
  • Give naloxone as soon as it is available

How to use naloxone nasal spray

One device equals one dose. If there is no response in 2 to 3 minutes, give another dose with a new device.

Call 911. If available, ask someone nearby to grab naloxone and an AED.
Lay on back, tilt head. Support the neck. Insert tip into a nostril until your fingers touch the nose.
Press plunger firmly to release the dose. Remove the device after spraying.
Start rescue breathing if not breathing normally. Give 1 breath every 5–6 seconds. If trained and needed, provide CPR.
Repeat in 2 to 3 minutes with a new device if there is little or no response or if breathing slows again.
Stay with the person until help arrives. Place them in the recovery position if they start breathing on their own.

Naloxone has a short effect. Symptoms can return. Monitor closely and be ready to give another dose.

Important
  • Naloxone will not harm someone if opioids are not present.
  • Store at room temperature. Check expiration dates.
  • Good Samaritan protections vary by state. Know your local policy.

Frequently asked questions

Can naloxone be used on someone who is not overdosing on opioids

Yes. Naloxone targets opioid receptors and will not harm someone if opioids are not involved.

How long does naloxone last

Its effects can wear off in 30 to 90 minutes. Breathing may slow again. Monitor and repeat doses as needed.

What should I expect after giving naloxone

The person may wake up disoriented or in withdrawal. Keep them safe, explain what happened, and wait for EMS.

Evidence and resources

Why this matters for UT students

  • About 1 in 5 students reported lifetime nonmedical use of at least one prescription medication class in a large multi campus study. Addictive Behaviors 2004.
  • Many students first opportunities to try prescription opioids occur after matriculation. College is a key window for initial exposure.
  • Family focused prevention ISFP in adolescence showed a 65 percent relative reduction in prescription opioid misuse by age 25. AJPH 2012.

These findings support campus level education, easy naloxone access, and evidence based prevention.
